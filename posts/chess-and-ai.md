I've played chess since I was a kid. And the more I learn about AI, the more I realize the two pursuits are in constant conversation with each other.

## Deep Blue to AlphaZero

Deep Blue beat Kasparov in 1997 using brute-force search — 200 million positions per second, hand-crafted evaluation functions, no learning. Then AlphaZero came along in 2017, taught itself chess from scratch in 4 hours using pure reinforcement learning, and became the strongest player in history.

The leap between those two systems is essentially the leap between classical AI and deep learning. Chess was the benchmark that forced that evolution.

## What Chess Teaches About Intelligence

Playing chess has given me a framework for thinking about thinking. Pattern recognition vs. calculation. Intuition vs. verification. When to go deep and when to trust your feel for the position. These are the same trade-offs that arise in neural architecture design.

## The Modern Lesson

AlphaZero plays moves humans find bizarre at first — long-term piece sacrifices, strange pawn structures. But they work. The model found patterns in the data that centuries of human play missed. That's the lesson for LLM research too: don't assume your human intuitions about what "should work" are correct. Let the data speak.
